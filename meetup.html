An Introduction to the Problem

Back To Top

Meetup.com is a popular platform for finding and attending events near you. People use it to deepen their professional skills, explore personal interests, and generally to socialize with people nearby that have similar interests.

Meetup’s tagline is “Find your people”, which I have done, however, after attending a few of these events, I realized that there isn’t a great way to stay connected to the people I meet at events. There is a minimal messaging system, but in order to connect with someone on a social network, I would have to remember their name or remember to get a business card.

If I fail at either, (which is likely to happen after meeting 20+ people) I could check the RSVP list on the event page on Meetup (easy, usually 25–30 people), but if that person didn’t RSVP, I would have to browse the member list (difficult, sometimes 1000+ members).

Once I succeeded at getting a name, I would then have to find them on Facebook and go through the process of adding them to the right list to hide personal information, or finding them on LinkedIn, where the UI is frightful and you are prompted at every other screen to connect with someone from your email contact list.

A Potential Solution

Back To Top

I sought to address this this multi-platform hullabaloo by bringing everything back into one platform. By allowing people to “friend” each other on Meetup, users would be encouraged to stay within the framework for events, networking, and communication. Having a profile would subsequently allow people to view their friends’ Meetup groups and interests, which would encourage exploration of interests one might not necessarily explore on his/her own.

Research (Surveys and Interviews)

Back To Top

Anonymous Survey

To validate this theory, we need some evidence. I started by recruiting volunteers across a variety of social networking platforms including Meetup, Facebook, Reddit, and LinkedIn. With 28 survey respondents and 5 interviewees, the data was eye-opening. Of those who took the survey, everyone had used some form of social network for longer than 3 years. Over 89% of respondents reported both using social media multiple times per day and using social networking to find an event at least once a month. That’s a lot of socializing!

Survey Results Infographic

Survey Results

Interviews for Qualitative Data

For my interviews, I chose mainly people who responded that they used or had heard of Meetup, and were in the demographic of social network users. All 4 of the 18–34 interviewees reported finding it difficult to meet new friends and socialize after college. Interestingly, the one older user (55–64) I interviewed said that she didn’t have a need nor want for more friends.

“I’m a little cautious about adding people to my Facebook… It’s very difficult to go back in and delete all these stupid things that have been posted over the years… So I decided to start fresh with a new Facebook account.”

Something that came up among all the younger (read: millenial) population was a nervousness or fear of new friends and professionals seeing incriminating photos that they might have been tagged in during their younger years. Some opted to create entirely new accounts or risk using Facebook by changing their security settings.

“I don’t even accept LinkedIn requests unless I can personally vouch for their professional qualifications.”

Another issue with connecting with people arose from LinkedIn. Two of the millenial interviewees reported feeling intimidated to start casual conversations on a “professional” platform, while the older respondent reported that she wouldn’t even connect with a person if she couldn’t vouch for his/her skill because she viewed the platform as a digital resumé.

Interview Takeaways

When I explored the issues further, I found that all the interviewees used social networks as vetting tools when meeting someone new and needed some background information. Existing platforms were either too personal or too professional. This is where Meetup can fill a niche opening in social networking with a casual, public-facing profile system that people will feel comfortable connecting with strangers on.

Heuristic Analysis

Back To Top

Ease of use is a huge factor in keeping users happy, and as such, comparing the existing platform with its competitors was important in the development of new features because users will already be familiar with their use.

Meetup.com

Meetup has some minor issues with its usability that break the pattern of general web use. For example, below, the widely accepted “logo in the top left is home” pattern is ignored, and has been placed in the middle of the navigation bar.

However, Meetup does have a strong match between other existing patterns, such as calendar view, page consistency across the site, and well-labeled actions and buttons. Finding and attending events is very easy with large buttons and accessible, understandable language.

Other Platforms

In order to gauge Meetup’s competitiveness with other platforms, I analyzed a few other well-known players, including Facebook and Eventbrite, as well as a lesser-known platform, CitySocializer.

Surprisingly, for as popular Facebook is among users, it has some poor heuristics in its events finding function. Most noticeably, navigating to different subcategories reveals inconsistent sort methods where the parameters for sorting were not immediately transparent.

Eventbrite, on the other hand, was very strong in error prevention and recovering from errors, and had strong flexibility for users by offering the ability to sort events in many different ways.

Empathy Maps, Personas, and Scenarios

Back To Top

Differentiating Users

After going through the data from the research, I found enough common features between the users to generate three different personas: Paul Professional, Sally Socializer, and Hank Hobbyist. Paul is someone who attends events mainly to expand his professional skills, Sally attends to see friends and explore her area, and Hank attends meetups to try out new hobbies.

Personas and Empathy Maps

Personas and Empathy Maps

Each of these personas actually stand to benefit from the introduction of an improved social platform. Paul can utilize the upgraded friend platform to explore skills and interests that pertain to his professional connections. Sally can share media from meetups with friends and other members, and Hank can explore hobbies that his friends are interested in.

A Minimum Viable Product

Back To Top

“As a user, I want to see other people’s interests so I can get to know them better and attend similar meetups.”

User Stories and the MVP

Once I discovered my Personas, I could use their motivations to guide what features an MVP would need, and how each persona would use them. All three personas would need to connect with other users at some point, so friending a person seemed to be the most relevant feature.

Following the “As a User, I want…” pattern, I came up with a variety of stories that each persona would follow, and then compared them against each other to come up with a list of features that would apply to all three personas. On this list was messaging ability, ability to view other user interests, a list of members attending, and a social wall or board for other users to post media.

Information Architecture and User Flows

Back To Top

Card Sorting for Clarity

After deciding on the features for the MVP, I had to organize where each feature should reside on the platform. To create cards, I selected user stories and converted them into simple actions. Then, I used existing locations on the website as the categories in which to sort. I recruited 4 people, within the age demographic, and both who had used and not used Meetup, to participate in a card sort. After the sort, I was able to decide where each feature would be found.

Card Sort

Creating the Sitemap and User Flows

Once I had my cards sorted, the sitemap came easily; I could immediately place the cards that everyone had agreed on, but for the cards where there had been disagreement, I based their locations on existing patterns that other social networking sites used.

Wireframes and Prototypes

Back To Top

Initial Wireframes

Because Meetup already has a basic profile, I started designing from the existing layout to facilitate a smooth transition and site homogeneity.

Initial Profile Layout, Left, and Revised Tabbed Layout, Right

Hand Drawn Wireframes

While the site map and user flows helped with the organization of what information should be on which page, the wireframing helped to organize the information on the page layout. Initially, I thought to show all the relevant information at once, but this approach resulted in a look that was too crowded and busy to easily discern what each subsection was.

Taking a page from Facebook, I opted for a tabbed approach to separate the distinct subsections of Group Memberships, Friends, Interests, and a Bulletin Board. This resulted in a cleaner, easily navigable layout that doesn’t distract the user’s eye.

Building a Prototype

Because this project was based on an existing platform, I was able to streamline much of the process by using screenshots of the existing platform and photoshopping in some of the features. Once they were edited, I imported them into Sketch to have a better overview of flow and composition of each page.

With the layouts completed and my wireframes converted to higher fidelity, I exported the boards to InVision to layout navigation using the web platform.

View Invision Prototype Here

One of the challenges I found when creating the prototype was providing enough functionality while still staying simple enough not to take all my time to add every feature. As a guideline and troubleshooting for this, I started by creating screens that were based on my user flows and adding more as necessary.

Testing the Prototype

Back To Top

Because my goal was to improve the social functioning of the platform, I tested users to see if the redesign was usable. As a result, I asked participants to 1) Find their profile, 2) Post media to a friend, 3) Find out what groups a friend was part of, 4) View the interests of a friend or stranger, and 5) View meetups based on a specific interest.

Initial Troubleshooting

“This is confusing. Why is there so much going on? This isn’t good.”

Unfortunately, testing the prototype did not go as smoothly as I had planned. I was unable to schedule participants who fit into the main demographics I had researched and who were current Meetup users. As a result, two of the subjects were immediately baffled by the landing page that would appear after a login and set a poor first impression in the test.

Learning from Errors

However, this confusion provided a great learning insight into first time users. All the participants, feeling confused by the landing page, headed to the search function first. By using the added universal search in the navigation bar, participants were able to easily accomplish all the tasks.

“Oh, this part is kind of like Facebook. That’s easy.”

Once the participants found the updated profile, which borrows patterns from existing social networks, the rest of the testing went relatively smoothly. The tabbed organization resulted in quick information retrieval as expected.

I had a bit of a learning curve when facilitating the testing, as I hadn’t thought that the order of tasks mattered as much until I noticed my first participant struggling with task 5, which I had initially placed as task 1. After my first test participant, I learned that the order of tasks should build from simple to complex, providing a scaffold for information. I hadn’t noticed this problem initially because I had worked so closely with the platform that I didn’t realize which actions users would find to be complex. After revising the order, the other two participants struggled far less.

Takeaways and Further Testing

Back To Top

In conclusion, the data indicates that there is a strong fit for an improved profile/person-to-person network. By implementing existing social network patterns for organizing profile data, Meetup could add a great deal of social functionality without much user confusion. By improving users’ ability to connect with other users, Meetup would encourage more time spent on the platform and promote platform activity by allowing users to explore more relevant meetup events.

Future Outcomes and Testing

I would like to revisit this project with an enhanced way of testing user activity. From what I could tell from testing, the prototype I presented didn’t have enough functionality to showcase Meetup’s ability to find and browse events, which I believe would lead to more appreciation of the social functions. I would also like to test the prototypes with people who have more experience with Meetup.com to see how well existing users would handle the implementation of the improved social system.
